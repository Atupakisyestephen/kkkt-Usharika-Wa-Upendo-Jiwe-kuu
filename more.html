a word about us

The Lutheran Church was founded in the early sixteenth century when a German monk, Martin Luther, boldly protested against the Roman Catholic Church's controversial practice of selling indulgences as a means of obtaining penance or atonement for sins committed against church teachings. Martin Luther's dissent sparked the Reformation, a pivotal movement that challenged the established norms and doctrines of the Catholic Church.

The theological principles of Lutheranism go beyond this historical context, encompassing the fundamental affirmations of classic Protestantism. Among these key tenets is the doctrine of "Sola Scriptura," emphasizing the centrality of the Bible as the sole authority in matters of faith and practice. Lutheranism also diverges from certain sacramental traditions upheld by the Catholic Church, rejecting five of the traditional seven sacraments.

This theological stance underscores Lutherans' distinct perspective on the means through which individuals connect with divine grace and the spiritual life. As Lutheranism evolved over the centuries, it has become a diverse and globally influential branch of Christianity, fostering a rich tapestry of traditions, beliefs, and practices.

Today, Lutherans worldwide continue to be guided by the enduring principles articulated by Martin Luther, cherishing a faith deeply rooted in scripture, grace, and the pursuit of a meaningful relationship with God.
